# Fault lines — the contested issues

> The issues likely to dominate negotiation, each linked to its [`provisions/`](provisions/) page.
> At this stage (proposal tabled, no co-legislator position) these are projected fault lines from
> early analyses and stakeholder reactions — recalibrate once Council/EP texts exist.

- **Assurance-level criteria vs the hyperscalers** ([sovereignty-framework](provisions/sovereignty-framework.md)) —
  levels 2–4 require third-party audits on EU personnel location, AI-training use of provider-generated
  data, supply-chain security and EU cybersecurity certification ("substantial"+); level 3 requires EU
  ownership and control. The Commission itself notes the **US CLOUD Act** blocks US providers from the
  highest levels. Analyses expect Member States with established hyperscaler relationships to pressure
  these criteria in negotiation. From the critical side, **Rikap & Stan** ([stakeholders](stakeholders.md))
  argue the operative test is *control* rather than *ownership*, leaving "the open window for Big Tech":
  a formally separated EU subsidiary with EU data location and EU staff could qualify. Note **Annex II is
  not transcribed** in this repo, so their reading and the reported "level 3 = EU ownership and control"
  cannot yet be reconciled against operative text — the criteria's exact wording is itself part of the
  fault line.
- **Hard obligations vs "à la carte"** ([public-sector-use](provisions/public-sector-use.md)) — the
  framework leaves room for national à-la-carte application rather than full harmonisation. MEP
  van Sparrentak (Greens/EFA) criticised the absence of hard obligations for governments to choose
  European cloud providers; the level-1 floor for non-public-order tasks is low.
- **Scope extension to the private sector** — the Commission's implementing-act power to require
  private NIS2-sector entities to run comparable assessments, and to designate third countries for
  framework assessment, is a sleeper sovereignty/trade issue (CLOUD Act / DPF tension; the Commission
  says the DPF "does not remove" operational-autonomy concerns).
- **Financing the ambition** — tripling data-centre capacity needs ~€200bn (mostly private); the
  package leans on a future "European equity capacity at scale" consultation and EIB instruments.
  Industry (incl. [DDA](stakeholders.md)) flags the contrast between strategic-autonomy ambition and
  permitting backlogs, grid congestion and regulatory burden.
- **Open source: principle vs investment** ([open-source](provisions/open-source.md)) — the
  open-source-first obligation is anchored in Arts 41–44, but the touted ~€2bn envelope (incl. an Open
  Source Maintenance Instrument) sits in the non-legislative Open Source Strategy; MEP Groothuis (VVD/
  Renew) criticised the lack of investment in European open-source AI models. **Caveat:** the €2bn
  figure circulates via secondary sources — unconfirmed in the official texts.
  A further critique from **Rikap & Stan** ([stakeholders](stakeholders.md)): openness of the models
  does not by itself shift rents, since inference on hyperscaler infrastructure is billed "be them open
  systems or not" — so open-source-first without infrastructure independence leaves the dependence intact.
- **Energy & sustainability of data centres** ([acceleration zones](provisions/data-centre-acceleration-zones.md)) —
  the announced Data Centre Energy Efficiency Package (rating scheme delegated act, possible minimum
  energy-performance standards) will interact with the acceleration-zone regime.
  **Rikap & Stan** ([stakeholders](stakeholders.md)) attack the tripling objective itself as accepting a
  "techno-solutionist narrative" — "trusting that innovation will compensate for the ecological footprint
  of data centers" — making the capacity target, not just its energy rules, contestable.
