## Assessment

**Confirmed against source.** Article by **Cecilia Rikap** (UCL, Institute for Innovation and Public
Purpose — Head of Research) and **Vali Stan** (Research Fellow, "AI for whom and for what?", Common
Wealth / UCL IIPP), Tech Policy Press, 29 June 2026.

- **In scope.** It discusses CADA directly and by substance: the **four assurance levels**, the
  **tripling of data-centre capacity by 2030**, **public-sector cloud procurement**, and the package's
  **open-source** preference. It does **not** discuss the `keep_apart` sibling (Chips Act 2.0,
  2026/0139) — the Chips file is not mentioned at all. The framing is package-level ("Tech Sovereignty
  Package") but the operative criticism is aimed at the CADA half.
- **Substantive, not commentary.** It is an academic/critical-political-economy position with a
  specific legal-design claim, not reportage of the proposal. Per the `stakeholder-social` playbook it
  therefore belongs in [`stakeholders.md`](../stakeholders.md), and — because it is a sharp
  civil-society/critical position on a contested design choice — it sharpens an existing entry in
  [`fault-lines.md`](../fault-lines.md).
- **The core claim, and a caveat.** The authors' central legal-design point is:
  > "The open window for Big Tech is the use of 'control' instead of ownership."
  i.e. that the framework's criteria turn on where a service is *controlled from* rather than who
  *owns* the provider, so a formally-separated EU subsidiary of a US hyperscaler can qualify. This
  **partly conflicts** with the repo's current (secondary-source) statement that "**Level 3 requires EU
  ownership and control**" ([sovereignty-framework](../provisions/sovereignty-framework.md)). The
  proposal's **Annex II** (the per-level criteria) is **not transcribed** in `extracts/` — the annexes
  live in a separate source document — so neither reading can be verified against operative text in
  this repo today. Record the authors' claim **as their position**, attributed and hedged; do **not**
  restate it as the proposal's content and do **not** amend the provision page's account of Level 3.
  (Note the operative Art 18 text *does* speak of providers "subject to the control of that third
  country", and only up to level 3 — consistent with the authors' focus on *control* as the operative
  test, see [Art 18](../../extracts/commission/COM-2026-502_sovereignty-framework-art16-28.md#article-18).)
- **Empirics are self-cited and unpublished.** The €10.8bn / 3,609 contracts figure (two-thirds
  Microsoft) is the authors' own **forthcoming** research; no external source is given. Attribute it to
  them explicitly and mark it unverified.
- **No new document to register.** Media/academic commentary published on a third-party site — not a
  primary government position document, no `data/documents.yaml` entry, nothing committed to
  `sources/`.
